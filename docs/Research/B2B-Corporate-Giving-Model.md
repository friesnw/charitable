# B2B: Corporate Donation Matching for Denver SMBs

*Research into the opportunity, business model, and path for GoodLocal to serve small Denver businesses that want to offer donation matching as an employee benefit.*

---

## The Core Opportunity in One Sentence

65% of Fortune 500 companies offer donation matching. Only 28% of small businesses do — not because they don't want to, but because every tool that exists was built for HR teams with $100K budgets. Nobody has built the lightweight, local-first version for a 75-person Denver agency.

---

## 1. Market Landscape

### Size of the Market

Corporate giving hit **$44.4 billion in 2024** (+9.1% YoY, a record). Matching gift programs specifically account for **$2–3 billion donated annually** — but an additional **$4–7 billion in eligible matching funds goes unclaimed every year** because employees don't know about the benefit or find submission too cumbersome. The problem isn't demand. It's broken participation infrastructure.

Over **26 million Americans** work for companies with matching programs. **78% of them don't know their company has one.**

### What Large Companies Use Today

| Platform | Target | Approx. Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Benevity** | Fortune 500 | $100K+/year | Dominant enterprise; inaccessible to SMBs |
| **YourCause (Blackbaud)** | Mid-to-large | Custom | Fast disbursement; no mobile app |
| **Bright Funds** | Mid-market | Custom | Consumer + workplace dual model — closest structural analog to GoodLocal |
| **Deed** | Tech, mission-driven | Custom (est. $10K–25K+) | YC-backed; polished UX; enterprise minimums |
| **Millie** | SMB to mid-market | Custom | "Dating app" discovery UX; strong employee experience |
| **Groundswell** | SMB to mid-market | **$4,500/year entry** | Most transparent SMB pricing; DAF-based; 1% distribution fee |
| **EarthShare** | Any size (environmental) | **$1–2 PEPM** | Cheapest in market; no local-first curation |
| **Double the Donation** | Nonprofits | $999–$30K/year | Nonprofit-facing, not employer-facing |

**The gap:** Benevity and YourCause are definitionally inaccessible to SMBs. Even Millie and Deed are $10K–$25K/year minimum. The only genuinely affordable options are Groundswell ($375/month) and EarthShare ($1–2 PEPM) — neither has a local-first angle.

**Nobody combines: affordable + zero admin + local-first curation.** That is the open lane.

## 2. Competitive Landscape

### Benevity

The dominant enterprise CSR platform. Used by ~1,000 companies including Google, Microsoft, and Nike. Covers donation matching, volunteer tracking, grant management, and cause marketing — the full corporate giving suite.

- **Pricing:** $100,000+/year; implementation fees on top; requires dedicated CSR administrator
- **Who it's for:** Fortune 500 and large enterprises with dedicated CSR teams and six-figure budgets
- **Strengths:** Deepest feature set in the market, largest nonprofit network (10,000+ verified orgs), white-label portals, enterprise-grade reporting
- **Weaknesses:** Completely inaccessible to SMBs in price, complexity, and sales motion; 1.5M nonprofits is overwhelming with no local curation; implementation takes months

**GoodLocal differentiation:** Not a competitor at all for SMBs. If anything, Benevity's dominance in enterprise creates the gap — SMBs can't use it and have nothing comparable at their price point.

---

### YourCause (Blackbaud)

The second-largest enterprise CSR platform, now part of Blackbaud (the dominant nonprofit software company). Strong in higher education, healthcare, and financial services.

- **Pricing:** Custom; generally lower than Benevity but still enterprise-minimum; no published pricing
- **Who it's for:** Mid-to-large enterprises already in the Blackbaud ecosystem
- **Strengths:** Fast fund disbursement via Blackbaud's existing nonprofit payment rails; AI-powered impact reporting; strong in sectors with large nonprofit relationships
- **Weaknesses:** No mobile app; requires Blackbaud relationship; same structural inaccessibility to SMBs as Benevity

**GoodLocal differentiation:** Same story as Benevity — a non-competitor for the SMB segment.

---

### Groundswell

The most direct SMB competitor and the clearest pricing benchmark. DAF (Donor-Advised Fund) based model — each employee gets a personal giving account funded by the employer match. Employees allocate from their DAF to any nonprofit.

- **Pricing:** $4,500/year (Benefactor, up to 200 employees) · $12,500/year (Philanthropist, adds volunteering) · 1% flat distribution fee on donations
- **Who it's for:** SMBs and mid-market companies wanting a simple, self-serve matching program
- **Strengths:** Most transparent pricing in the category; SOC 2 compliant; clean employee UX; DAF model means tax advantages for employer; no per-transaction complexity
- **Weaknesses:** National focus with no local curation (employees choose from any nonprofit, which reintroduces choice paralysis); no community identity story; still $375/month which is high for a 30-person company

**GoodLocal differentiation:** Price below ($299/month entry vs. $375/month), and offer what Groundswell can't — a curated local roster that makes the matching benefit feel like a Denver community statement, not just a checkbox. The "Give Where You Live" identity narrative is entirely absent from Groundswell.

---

### Millie

The most consumer-forward workplace giving tool in the SMB space. Known for a "dating app" style nonprofit discovery experience — employees get matched to 3 nonprofits per week based on their location and interests. Founded in 2018, bootstrapped.

- **Pricing:** Custom; no published pricing — estimated $800–$2,000+/month based on market positioning and feature set
- **Who it's for:** SMBs and mid-market companies that want a branded, employee-experience-first matching program
- **Strengths:** Best employee UX in the category; local discovery is a native feature (location-aware); strong onboarding and employee activation focus; good for employer brand storytelling
- **Weaknesses:** Custom pricing creates friction in the sales process; no Denver-specific curation; national focus means the "local" story is generic; higher price floor than Groundswell

**GoodLocal differentiation:** Millie is the most similar in spirit — both prioritize employee experience and discovery. GoodLocal's edge is Denver specificity. Millie's "local" is algorithmic and national; GoodLocal's is hand-curated and community-embedded. GoodLocal can also undercut on price significantly if Millie's estimates are accurate.

---

### Deed

A YC-backed workplace giving platform that combines donation matching, volunteering, and skills-based giving. Targets tech companies and mission-driven employers.

- **Pricing:** Custom; 2.5% disbursement fee on all donations; estimated $15,000–$40,000/year ACV; enterprise minimums
- **Who it's for:** Tech companies (especially post-Series A startups and public tech companies) that want a full employee engagement suite
- **Strengths:** Slick, modern UX; covers volunteering alongside giving; strong brand in tech circles; good API for HRIS integration
- **Weaknesses:** 2.5% disbursement fee conflicts with "we don't touch the money" positioning; enterprise-focused pricing; no local-first angle; volunteering focus adds complexity SMBs don't need

**GoodLocal differentiation:** Deed's 2.5% fee on donations is a philosophical and marketing liability GoodLocal can exploit. "100% of your match goes to the charity — we charge for the platform, not the philanthropy" is a direct counter-positioning. Deed also skews expensive and complex for what most SMBs need.

---

### EarthShare

The most affordable workplace giving tool in the market, with a focus on environmental nonprofits. Charges $1–2 per employee per month, with the fee deducted from payouts rather than invoiced upfront.

- **Pricing:** $1–2 PEPM; fee deducted from donations (so no upfront cost to employer); no implementation fee
- **Who it's for:** Any size company, especially those with environmental values; large employee bases where PEPM is a natural fit
- **Strengths:** Lowest price in the market; no invoicing friction (fee deducted from payouts); accessible to any company size; no minimum commitment
- **Weaknesses:** Environmental sector focus limits applicability; no local curation; limited employer dashboard and reporting; minimal brand presence in Denver market

**GoodLocal differentiation:** EarthShare is cheap but narrow. GoodLocal's flat-fee SaaS model is more predictable for employers, and the local curation is the product — not an afterthought. At 50 employees, EarthShare would cost $600–$1,200/year but offer essentially no curation or community story. GoodLocal at $299/month ($3,588/year) is more expensive but a fundamentally different product.

## 3. Denver Small Business Landscape

### The Numbers

Colorado has ~675,000 small businesses — 99.5% of all companies in the state, ranked 5th nationally by proportion. Denver is the dominant hub. There are thousands of firms in the 50–200 employee band across the metro.

### Which Industries Are the Best Fit

In priority order for GoodLocal's sales motion:

1. **Tech startups and agencies** — Culture-driven, competing on employer brand, many already in Pledge 1% or B Corp. "Local giving as culture" fits their recruiting narrative naturally.
2. **B Corps and purpose-driven businesses** — Already ideologically aligned. Colorado has 100+ certified B Corps.
3. **Professional services** (law, accounting, design, marketing) — Community reputation is part of their business development model.
4. **Real estate and property management** — Local reputation is the core asset; community giving is a natural brand extension.
5. **Food, beverage, and hospitality** — Neighborhood-identity-oriented; "Denver-first" framing is native to their brand.

### Denver-Specific Sales Assets

**Pledge 1% Colorado** — 250+ member companies who have publicly committed to giving as corporate identity. This is GoodLocal's highest-quality cold outreach list — companies that have already decided "giving is part of who we are" and just need the operational tool. Pledge 1% Colorado has generated $7.5M in community funding for Colorado nonprofits.

**B Local Colorado** — 100+ certified B Corps in the regional community. B Corp certification includes "workers" and "community" standards that donation matching directly supports. Events and community touchpoints are direct, warm sales access.

**Built In Colorado** — Denver's tech employer brand platform. Companies here are explicitly competing on perks and culture. "Donation matching via GoodLocal" as a listed perk is both product placement and a sales signal.

## 3. What Employees Actually Want

### Generational Data

Millennials and Gen Z are now **54% of the U.S. workforce** and rising to ~80% by 2030. For this cohort:

- **74% of Millennials** identify as philanthropists (vs. 35% of Baby Boomers)
- **75% of Gen Z and Millennials** say community engagement is an important factor when evaluating an employer
- **71% of employees** say it's imperative or very important that their company culture supports giving
- Gen Z donors averaged **$867/year** in contributions; Millennials averaged **$1,616** — both surpassing Gen X
- More than **half of both generations** prefer monthly giving over annual campaigns
- They're **73% more likely than Baby Boomers** to value recognition for charitable participation

For the companies GoodLocal wants to sell to — Denver tech, agencies, professional services — this demographic *is* the workforce. A donation matching benefit isn't a nice-to-have; it shows up directly in recruiting conversations.

### Retention ROI

- Strong CSR programs can reduce turnover by **up to 50%** among employees who participate
- At a 100-person company with $120K average fully-loaded employee cost, retaining even 1–2 extra employees per year saves $30K–$60K
- A $6,000–$9,000/year GoodLocal subscription pays for itself if it contributes to even marginal retention improvement

### Why Local Framing Matters for Engagement

- Employees are more motivated by organizations that affect "their families, neighbors, and schools"
- **86% of corporate leaders** believe employees expect them to engage with the local community
- Allowing employees to direct matching dollars to local charities adds agency that national programs lack

An employee seeing their match go to a Denver food bank, visible in the GoodLocal app with active status, has a fundamentally different emotional experience than scrolling a generic Benevity dropdown of 1.5 million nonprofits. **Local = legible = motivating.**

### Common Friction Points That Kill Corporate Matching Programs

1. Administrative overhead — manual verification, tracking, and payouts
2. Choice paralysis — no curated list means employees don't use it, or HR has to vet charities themselves
3. Budget uncertainty — CFOs nervous about open-ended matching commitments
4. Low awareness — 78% of employees at matching companies don't know about the benefit
5. Cumbersome submission — employees abandon the process; billions go unclaimed

GoodLocal's product design addresses all five: curated Denver list, automated tracking, per-employee caps, employer dashboard, employee visibility through the existing consumer app.

## 4. Business Model

### Pricing Structure

Avoid any model that takes a cut of donated dollars. "We don't touch the money" is a genuine trust differentiator and must be preserved in the B2B model exactly as it is in the consumer product. Charge for the platform; every dollar of the matching fund goes to the charity via Every.org.

**Suggested tiers:**

| Tier | Price | Size | Included |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Starter** | $299/month | Up to 50 employees | Curated nonprofit roster, employer dashboard, per-employee cap controls |
| **Growth** | $499/month | 51–150 employees | + Quarterly impact reports, co-branded employee portal |
| **Scale** | $749/month | 151–300 employees | + Custom nonprofit curation, priority support |

This puts GoodLocal:
- Below Groundswell's entry tier ($375/month)
- Well below Millie and Deed (estimated $800–$2,000+/month)
- Differentiated from EarthShare by local curation and better UX

### Revenue Range Per Customer

For a typical 100-person Denver company:
- Platform fee: ~$499–$749/month = **$6,000–$9,000/year ACV**
- Employer matching fund (flows through Every.org, not GoodLocal revenue): $50–$100/employee/year = $5,000–$10,000/year
- Total cost to employer: $11,000–$19,000/year — a real but reasonable benefits line item

**ARR scenarios:**
- 10 customers: $60K–$90K ARR
- 25 customers: $150K–$225K ARR
- 50 customers: $300K–$450K ARR — fundable, meaningful traction

## 5. Sales Approach

### Who Is the Buyer

| Company Size | Primary Buyer | Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Under 75 employees | CEO or founder | Office Manager |
| 75–200 employees | Head of People / HR Manager | CEO signs off |

Under 100 employees, this is a one-meeting, demo, 30-day-trial, credit-card close. Not enterprise procurement. The motion is closer to selling Gusto than Workday. Sales cycle target: **2–4 weeks**.

### Best Sales Channels

1. **Pledge 1% Colorado (250 companies)** — Highest-quality warm list in the state. Founder-to-founder outreach. Explore formal partnership or co-marketing.
2. **B Local Colorado (100+ B Corps)** — Attend events; GoodLocal directly supports B Corp "community" certification standards.
3. **Built In Colorado** — Get listed as a company perk; direct outreach to HR leads at listed companies.
4. **HR consultants and PEOs** — TriNet, Justworks, and Gusto serve Denver SMBs; getting listed as a recommended add-on creates distribution with no direct sales overhead.
5. **Denver Metro Chamber of Commerce** — Lower conversion rate than Pledge 1%, but broader reach across industries.

### Land and Expand

- **Land:** Starter plan. Employer offers matching to all employees against a curated list of 15–20 Denver nonprofits.
- **Expand within account:** Upgrade to Growth as headcount grows; add co-branded portal; add campaign features (employee-nominated charities, team giving challenges).
- **Expand via network:** Each company is a reference. CEO-to-CEO word of mouth is dense in the 50–200-person Denver company ecosystem.
- **Consumer flywheel:** B2B employees who use GoodLocal for their employer benefit may become personal consumer users. Every B2B customer seeds consumer growth — and vice versa.

## 6. Strategic Considerations

### The Risk: Attention Dilution

B2B requires a different buyer persona, outbound sales motion, support SLAs, and stability focus vs. consumer feature velocity. For a small team pursuing both simultaneously, the risk is building neither well.

**The counterargument:** GoodLocal's consumer product — the nonprofit discovery interface, trust explanations, Every.org donation flow — is already the employee-facing interface a B2B product would need. The B2B layer is an addition to existing infrastructure, not a greenfield build.

### Analogous Companies

**Bright Funds** — Built consumer giving first, then added a workplace module. Consumer credibility gave it employee-side trust; B2B module gave it revenue. Most direct structural analog.

**Millie** — B2B-first, but the consumer experience (employees get a personal app) is the core product UX. Employer pays; employee uses the app. Same structure as GoodLocal would use.

**Pattern across all:** Consumer and B2B are most compatible when the consumer product *is* the employee-facing interface for the B2B product. That is exactly GoodLocal's situation.

### Minimum Viable B2B Feature Set (Pilot with 3–5 Companies)

Required for a functional pilot:

1. **Employer admin dashboard** — Set per-employee annual cap, view consumed vs. remaining matching budget
2. **Employee invitation flow** — Admin sends invite link; employees link their GoodLocal account (or create one)
3. **Curated local roster** — 15–25 pre-vetted Denver nonprofits; employer can optionally restrict
4. **Matching tracking** — Employee donates via GoodLocal; system flags for match; employer approves (manual is fine for pilot)
5. **Impact summary** — Monthly email/dashboard: total matched, participation rate, nonprofits funded
6. **Every.org integration** — Donations flow via existing widget; matching tracked in GoodLocal DB

Not required for pilot: payroll integration, white-labeling, advanced APIs, volunteer tracking, grant management.

**Estimated build scope:** 4–8 focused development weeks, using GoodLocal's existing Every.org integration, auth system, and charity database as foundations.

## 7. Phased Path

**Phase 0 — Now:** Consumer-only. Donors discover Denver nonprofits, donate via Every.org, track giving history.

**Phase 1 — Validation (months 1–2):** Identify 5–10 Pledge 1% / B Corp companies. Founder-to-founder conversations. Validate: Would they pay $300–$500/month? What's the minimum feature set? What's their biggest fear?

**Phase 2 — Manual Pilot (months 3–5):** Sign 3–5 pilot companies at reduced/free rate. Build minimum employer dashboard. Operate matching verification manually. Measure: employee activation rate, donation amounts, employer NPS, renewal intent.

**Phase 3 — Paid B2B Launch (month 6+):** With pilot proof, launch paid tiers. Formalize pricing. Build self-serve onboarding. Warm outreach to Pledge 1% Colorado and B Local Colorado.

**Phase 4 — Channel Partnerships (months 9–12):** Approach PEOs, Denver Chamber, HR consultants. Explore revenue-share referral arrangements. At 20–30 customers, traction exists to attract channel partners.

**Phase 5 — Consumer-B2B Flywheel:** B2B employees become personal GoodLocal users. Consumer retention and B2B renewal reinforce each other. GoodLocal is where Denver gives — whether you found it through your employer or on your own.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The gap is real and structural.** Enterprise tools are inaccessible to SMBs. Lightweight alternatives have no local-first angle. Nobody has combined affordable + zero admin + local curation.

2. **Pledge 1% Colorado (250 companies) is the first-customer pool.** Pre-qualified, warm, already committed. Don't cold-pitch generic chamber members until you have 10 referenceable customers.

3. **Price below Groundswell ($4,500/year).** Enter at $299/month (~$3,600/year). Beat them on local relevance and simplicity.

4. **Buyer is one person, not a committee.** CEO or Head of People. Under $10K/year, no procurement. Two-to-four week sales cycle with a 30-day pilot offer.

5. **Build the pilot before building the product.** 3–5 companies, operated manually first. The employer dashboard should be shaped by real usage.

6. **Never take a cut of donated dollars.** Platform SaaS fee only. Every dollar of the match fund flows through Every.org to the charity. The trust positioning must be preserved in the B2B model exactly as in the consumer product.

7. **Consumer and B2B are the same product.** The consumer app is the employee-facing interface. Sequence the build — consumer credibility first, then B2B pilot — but treat them as one coherent system.
