# GoodLocal B2B Research: Corporate Donation Matching for Denver SMBs

**Research date:** 2026-02-23
**Purpose:** Evaluate a B2B business model where small-to-mid-size Denver businesses offer donation matching as an employee benefit, powered by GoodLocal.

---

## 1. Market Landscape — Corporate Donation Matching

### Market Size

Corporate giving reached **$44.4 billion in 2024**, the highest level on record, representing 9.1% growth. Of that total, matching gift programs specifically account for an estimated **$2–3 billion donated annually** — but critically, an additional **$4–7 billion in available matching funds goes unclaimed every year** because employees don't know how to submit or don't bother. That gap is the market opportunity: it's not awareness of matching that's lacking, it's participation infrastructure.

The broader employee giving + volunteering market that platforms like Benevity and Millie operate in is part of the corporate social responsibility (CSR) software category, valued at several billion dollars globally and growing.

### What Large Companies Use Today

The enterprise-dominant platforms are:

| Platform | Target Market | Estimated Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Benevity** | Large enterprise (1,000+ employees) | $100,000+/year | Highest cost in market; 15+ years of history; largest client base; highest ease-of-use scores |
| **YourCause (Blackbaud)** | Mid-to-large enterprise | Variable; lower than Benevity | AI-powered impact reporting; no mobile app; faster fund disbursement via Blackbaud network |
| **Bright Funds** | Mid-market | Custom/not published | Dual model (corporate + individual giving); workplace-first positioning |
| **Deed** | Tech companies, mission-driven employers | Custom; 2.5% disbursement fees | Y Combinator-backed; slick UX; no published pricing |
| **Millie** | SMB to mid-market | Custom quote; not published | Closest to GoodLocal's potential market; "dating app" style nonprofit discovery; 3 nonprofits/week matched to employee location and preferences |
| **Groundswell** | SMB to mid-market | **$4,500/year (Benefactor), $12,500/year (Philanthropist)** | Published pricing — most transparent in category; 1% flat distribution fee; DAF-based giving accounts; SOC 2 compliant |
| **EarthShare** | Any size | **$1–2 PEPM** (per employee per month) | Environmental focus; no implementation fee; fee deducted from payouts |
| **Double the Donation** | Nonprofits (helps them surface matches) | $999–$30,000/year (nonprofit-facing) | Not an employer-side tool; helps nonprofit identify eligible employee matches |

**Key insight:** Benevity and YourCause are definitionally not built for small businesses. Their sales processes, implementation requirements, and pricing tiers ($100K+/year) lock out any company under ~500 employees. Even Millie and Deed use custom pricing (a sales signal of $10,000+ ACV minimums and complex implementation). The only platforms with genuinely SMB-accessible pricing are Groundswell and EarthShare — both of which lack local-first framing.

### The Small Business Gap

**65% of Fortune 500 companies** offer matching gifts. Only **28% of small-to-mid-size companies** do.

The reasons the enterprise tools fail SMBs are structural:
1. **Pricing** — Benevity implementations frequently exceed what an SMB pays in total SaaS annually
2. **Implementation complexity** — Enterprise CSR platforms require dedicated HR or CSR staff to administer; most SMBs have no such role
3. **Overkill feature sets** — Volunteer tracking, grant management, custom reporting APIs, white labeling — none of this is relevant to a 60-person agency trying to offer $50/employee/year in donation matching
4. **Sales motion** — Enterprise tools require multi-month sales cycles with procurement, legal, and security review; an SMB founder can't navigate this
5. **Minimum viable budgets** — Even "mid-market" pricing at $10K–$25K/year is prohibitive for a 75-person company

**What small businesses want:** A simple, trustworthy way to offer matching as a benefit — ideally something employees can actually use, that generates a social media moment or a team culture artifact, without requiring an HR administrator to manage it month-to-month.

No published data exists on "% of SMBs that want to offer matching but don't," but the 65% vs. 28% gap in adoption between large and small companies is itself the signal. The demand is there; the accessible tool isn't.

### Lightweight / SMB-Focused Competitors

- **Groundswell**: The most transparent pricing in the category. $4,500/year entry tier. DAF-based (each employee gets a giving account). 1% distribution fee. No ad hoc fees. Currently the best option for SMBs, but no local-first angle and positioned nationally.
- **Millie**: Custom pricing but with SMB-friendly messaging. Discovery feature (3 nonprofits/week) is the most compelling employee-facing feature in the category. No published pricing is a friction point.
- **EarthShare**: $1–2 PEPM is the most affordable structure in the market. Environmental focus limits cross-sector appeal. Fee deducted from payouts rather than charged upfront — a genuinely SMB-friendly model.
- **Percent Pledge**: Custom pricing; positions as "easy giving for any company size"; limited public data.

**The gap GoodLocal can fill:** A local-first matching benefit designed specifically for Denver companies. Sub-$500/month. No implementation project. Curated local nonprofit roster (not 1.5M choices that overwhelm). Turns the matching benefit into a community-identity story ("we give to Denver").

## 2. Denver Small Business Landscape

### Size of the Market

Colorado has approximately **674,741 small businesses**, representing 99.5% of all businesses in the state. Colorado ranks 5th nationally for the highest proportion of small businesses.

The Denver metro area is the state's dominant business hub. Precise counts of Denver-specific businesses in the 50–200 employee range aren't publicly broken down, but the U.S. Census Bureau's County Business Patterns data for Denver-Aurora-Lakewood MSA suggests several thousand companies in that size band across all industries.

### Key Industries

Colorado's SMB economy is led by:
1. **Professional, scientific, and technical services** (largest sector by count; 118,000+ small firms statewide)
2. **Construction**
3. **Health care and social assistance**
4. **Retail trade**
5. **Food and beverage** (3,200 production-related companies in Colorado)
6. **Financial services**
7. **Tech/fintech** (180+ fintech companies in Metro Denver alone)

### Industries Most Likely to Care About Local Giving as a Benefit

In priority order for GoodLocal's sales motion:

1. **Tech startups and agencies** — Culture-driven, competing for talent, many are already Pledge 1% members or B Corps. The "local giving as culture" narrative resonates with their employer brand.
2. **B Corps and purpose-driven businesses** — Already ideologically aligned. Certified B Corps in Colorado number 100+, concentrated in Denver and Boulder. Notable Denver-area B Corps: CauseLabs (tech), Techstars (startup accelerator), Namaste Solar, Apollo Energy.
3. **Professional services firms** (law, accounting, consulting, design/marketing agencies) — Community ties matter for business development; local giving reinforces client relationships in the Denver market.
4. **Real estate and property management** — Local reputation is their core asset; community giving is a natural marketing extension.
5. **Food, beverage, and hospitality** — Already oriented around neighborhood identity; "Denver-first" framing maps directly onto their brand.

### Denver-Specific Context

**B Corp density:** Colorado has 100+ certified B Corps, one of the highest concentrations in the U.S. B Local Colorado is the regional B Corp community organization — both a sales channel and a social proof network. Being used by B Local Colorado members creates credibility that flows downstream.

**Pledge 1% Colorado:** A local chapter of the Pledge 1% movement, currently representing 250+ Colorado companies who have pledged equity, profit, employee time, or product to nonprofits. No fees to participate. This network is a direct warm outreach list for GoodLocal — these companies have explicitly opted into "giving as corporate identity" and would be receptive to a tool that makes donation matching operationally easy.

**Giving culture:** Denver's philanthropic ecosystem is unusually stable because the local economy is not reliant on a single industry. Corporate giving programs have access to a stable variety of sectors. The Denver Foundation, Colorado Gives Day, and local community foundations represent a mature local nonprofit ecosystem that GoodLocal's curated list could draw from.

**Built In Colorado** profiles corporate giving at Denver tech companies extensively (HomeAdvisor, Scaled Agile, Conga), indicating that local giving is a meaningful employer brand signal in the Denver tech market specifically.

## 3. Employee Benefits and Giving — What Employees Actually Want

### Generational Attitudes

Millennials and Gen Z now represent **54% of the U.S. workforce** (Bureau of Labor Statistics projects Gen Z alone will be 30% by 2030). Their attitudes toward workplace giving are meaningfully different from prior generations:

- **74% of Millennials** identify as philanthropists (vs. 35% of Baby Boomers)
- **75% of Gen Z and Millennials** say an organization's community engagement and societal impact are important factors when considering a potential employer
- **71% of employees** say it is imperative or very important to work where the culture supports giving and volunteering
- **Gen Z donors** averaged $867/year in charitable contributions in 2024; Millennials averaged $1,616 — both surpassing Gen X's giving levels
- **73% more likely** than Baby Boomers to value recognition for charitable participation
- More than **half of both Gen Z and Millennials** prefer monthly giving arrangements (subscription-style), not annual campaigns

These numbers mean: for the demographic cohort that dominates hiring in Denver's tech and professional services sectors, a donation matching benefit is not a "nice to have" — it's a visible signal of employer values.

### Recruitment and Retention Data

- **77% of employers** believe offering employee engagement opportunities is an important recruitment strategy to attract Millennials
- A well-designed CSR program can increase **employee engagement by up to 7.5%**, **productivity by 13%**, and **reduce turnover by up to 50%**
- Companies with strong social impact initiatives see up to **50% lower turnover** among employees who participate in giving programs
- The 2014 Millennial Impact Study found that **53% of Millennials** cite having their passions recognized as the top reason for staying at their current company

**The ROI case is strong:** For a 100-person company with average fully-loaded employee cost of $100,000–$150,000, reducing turnover by even 1–2 employees saves $30,000–$60,000 in recruiting and onboarding costs. A $5,000–$10,000/year matching program that contributes to retention pays for itself many times over.

### Friction Points for Implementation

The most commonly cited barriers for SMBs trying to implement matching:

1. **Administrative overhead** — Manual verification of employee donations, tracking match requests, cutting checks to nonprofits
2. **Choice paralysis** — No curated list means employees either don't use the benefit or HR has to build their own vetting process
3. **Budget uncertainty** — Without a cap or per-employee limit, CFOs are nervous about open-ended matching commitments
4. **Low awareness** — **78% of employees** at companies with matching programs don't know their company offers it
5. **Cumbersome submission process** — Many employees find the process too complex and abandon it; billions go unclaimed as a result

GoodLocal's product design can address all five: automated matching workflows, curated Denver nonprofit roster, per-employee annual caps, employer dashboard with clear budget tracking, and employee-facing visibility into the benefit.

### Local vs. National Framing

There is a clear trend toward localized, hyperlocal giving as a driver of engagement. Key signals:
- Companies are increasingly focusing volunteer and matching efforts on "the communities where employees live and work"
- Employees are more motivated to give to organizations they know personally — ones that affect "their families, neighbors, and schools"
- **86% of corporate leaders** believe employees expect them to engage with the local community
- Allowing employees to vote on local charities to support adds engagement and agency that national programs lack

**For GoodLocal specifically:** The "local" framing is a genuine differentiation, not just a marketing claim. An employee who sees their matching dollars going to a Denver food bank, a local youth arts program, or a neighborhood shelter — and can see on the GoodLocal app that it's active and giving — has a meaningfully different emotional experience than clicking through a generic Benevity dropdown of 1.5 million nonprofits. Local = legible = motivating.

## 4. B2B Business Model Options

### Viable Pricing Models

| Model | Description | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Per seat (PEPM)** | Charge per employee per month | Predictable, scales with company size, industry standard for HR benefits | Can feel expensive for small headcount; need minimum thresholds |
| **Flat monthly/annual SaaS** | Fixed fee by tier based on employee band | Simple to sell, simple to budget | Revenue doesn't grow with usage |
| **% of matched donations** | Take a cut of every dollar matched | Revenue scales with engagement | Conflicts with "separation of incentives" principle; feels like taking from the charity |
| **Platform fee per transaction** | Small fee on each donation processed | Usage-based; aligns with value delivered | Hard to predict; conflicts with trust positioning |
| **Hybrid: flat SaaS + PEPM** | Base platform fee + per active employee | Balances predictability with scale | Slightly more complex to explain |

**Recommendation for GoodLocal:** The PEPM model or flat annual tiers are the most compatible with the trust positioning and the simplest to sell to SMB buyers. A **% of matched donations** model should be avoided — it would undermine the "we don't touch the money" principle and is philosophically misaligned with how GoodLocal has positioned itself.

A clean structure could be:
- **Starter:** $299/month (up to 50 employees) — includes curated local nonprofit roster, employer dashboard, per-employee match cap controls
- **Growth:** $499/month (51–150 employees) — everything in Starter + impact reporting, co-branded employee portal
- **Scale:** $749/month (151–300 employees) — everything in Growth + custom nonprofit curation, priority support

These numbers put GoodLocal in a competitive range: cheaper than Groundswell's $4,500/year ($375/month) entry tier, more than EarthShare's $1–2 PEPM at small headcount, and dramatically cheaper than Millie or Benevity at any tier.

### Competitive Pricing Reference

- **Groundswell Benefactor:** $4,500/year ($375/month) — 10–200 employees
- **Groundswell Philanthropist:** $12,500/year ($1,042/month) — adds volunteering
- **EarthShare:** $1–2 PEPM — scales down as program grows; fee deducted from payouts
- **Millie:** Custom — likely $10,000–$25,000/year based on SMB market positioning
- **Benevity:** $100,000+/year — inaccessible to SMBs
- **Deed:** Custom; 2.5% disbursement fee — likely $15,000–$40,000/year

### Revenue Range Per Customer

For a 100-person Denver company:
- **GoodLocal pricing:** ~$499–$749/month = **$6,000–$9,000/year ACV**
- **Matching budget sponsored by employer:** $50/employee/year cap = $5,000/year in matched donations (flows through Every.org, not GoodLocal's margin)
- **Total cost to employer:** $6,000–$9,000 (platform) + $5,000 (match fund) = **$11,000–$14,000/year** — a very reasonable benefits line item

At **10 customers,** GoodLocal earns $60,000–$90,000 ARR from platform fees alone.
At **25 customers,** $150,000–$225,000 ARR.
At **50 customers,** $300,000–$450,000 ARR — meaningful, fundable traction.

### Typical Sales Cycle and ACV for SMB HR Benefits

- SMB HR SaaS ACV typically ranges from **$5,000–$15,000/year**
- Sales cycles are **2–8 weeks** (vs. 6+ months for enterprise)
- Decision is often made by a single person (see Section 5)
- Free trials or 30/60-day pilots are standard for SMB tools and accelerate conversion
- Many SMB SaaS companies see a credit card or single PO — no procurement process required under a certain threshold (typically $10,000–$15,000)

## 5. Sales Approach for SMBs

### Who Is the Buyer

At a 50–150 person company, the decision for a tool like this is most likely made by:

1. **CEO or founder** (primary buyer for companies under 75 people) — motivated by employer brand, culture, talent strategy; can sign off on $5,000–$10,000/year without procurement
2. **Head of People / HR Manager** (primary buyer for 75–200 person companies) — looking for benefit differentiation for recruiting; wants something that "just works" with no admin burden
3. **Office Manager / Operations Manager** (common in agencies, professional services) — often owns "culture and perks" budget; practically-minded, will buy on ease of use

**Implication:** This is not an enterprise procurement sale. It's closer to selling Gusto or Rippling than selling Workday. One meeting, a demo, a 30-day pilot, a credit card.

### Effective Sales Channels

1. **Pledge 1% Colorado network** — 250+ member companies in Colorado who have already opted into "giving as corporate identity." A warm, pre-qualified list. GoodLocal should approach Pledge 1% Colorado about a formal partnership or co-marketing arrangement.

2. **B Local Colorado** — The B Corp community in Colorado. These companies are already meeting high social impact standards. Offering a product that makes donation matching easy aligns perfectly with their certification values. Annual B Corp conference and community events are direct sales touchpoints.

3. **Denver Metro Chamber of Commerce** — Access to member companies across all industries. Events, directory listings, and sponsored content. Lower conversion rate than Pledge 1% but much larger audience.

4. **Built In Colorado** — Employer brand platform popular with Denver tech companies. Companies that list on Built In are specifically competing for tech talent and care about perks/culture messaging. GoodLocal as a listed employer perk ("donation matching via GoodLocal") is both a product placement and a sales signal.

5. **HR consultants and PEOs (Professional Employer Organizations)** — PEOs like TriNet, Insperity, and Justworks already bundle benefits for SMBs. Getting listed as a recommended add-on within a PEO's partner ecosystem creates a distribution channel without direct sales overhead. Colorado-specific HR consultants who advise on benefits packages are another warm channel.

6. **Direct outreach to Pledge 1% members and B Corps** — A targeted, personalized cold email sequence to the 250 Pledge 1% companies and 100+ B Corps in Colorado is the highest-leverage first sales motion. These are people who have publicly committed to corporate giving; GoodLocal is an offer to make that commitment operationally real.

7. **Tech4Good Denver** — A community connecting Denver's tech sector and nonprofit community. A natural audience for both sides of GoodLocal's platform.

### Land and Expand

In a GoodLocal B2B context, "land and expand" looks like:

- **Land:** Sign a company on the Starter tier ($299/month). They offer matching to all employees, curated to 10–15 local nonprofits.
- **Expand (within account):** Upgrade to Growth as headcount grows; add co-branded portal; add campaign features (employee-nominated nonprofits, giving challenges).
- **Expand (network):** Each participating company is a reference customer. The CEO who loves GoodLocal tells two other CEOs. The Head of People mentions it at a Denver HR roundtable.
- **Expand (platform):** If GoodLocal maintains the consumer app, B2B employees who engage with the matching benefit may become personal GoodLocal users — a flywheel between the consumer and B2B products.

### Common Objections and Responses

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "We already have a matching program — we just do it manually" | Manual matching means 78% of eligible donations go unclaimed. GoodLocal handles the workflow so employees actually use the benefit. |
| "We can't afford to match right now" | GoodLocal works with whatever budget you set — $25/employee/year is a real benefit. You control the cap. |
| "We're too small for this to matter" | At 50 employees, a $5,000 matching fund turns into $10,000+ in local nonprofit impact and shows up on every recruiting call. |
| "Our employees are spread across the country" | GoodLocal's Denver-focused curation is a differentiator, not a limitation. If you're a Denver company, your local giving matters to Denver employees and clients alike. |
| "We don't have someone to manage this" | That's the point. GoodLocal takes 20 minutes to set up and runs itself. The employer dashboard shows you what's happening without requiring anyone to administer it. |
| "What happens if a nonprofit we're matching to closes?" | GoodLocal monitors nonprofit status continuously. If a nonprofit closes or loses eligibility, we notify you and suggest alternatives. |

## 6. Strategic Considerations

### Risks of a B2B Addition to a Consumer Product

The central risk is **attention dilution.** A B2B product has fundamentally different requirements from a consumer product:

- Different buyer persona (HR / CEO vs. individual donor)
- Different sales motion (outbound prospecting vs. organic consumer growth)
- Different support requirements (SLAs, onboarding, account management vs. self-serve)
- Different product velocity (enterprise wants stability; consumers want new features)
- Different revenue model (annual contracts vs. consumer monetization, which GoodLocal doesn't yet have)

For a two-person team, pursuing both aggressively simultaneously is a recipe for building neither well. The risk is not that the B2B model is wrong — it may actually be the more viable revenue path — but that it pulls engineering and founder time away from consumer product-market fit before that's been established.

The counterargument: GoodLocal's consumer product is *already the discovery and giving interface* that a B2B product would need. The nonprofit database, the trust signals, the donation flow via Every.org — these are shared infrastructure. The B2B product is a layer on top of the consumer product, not a separate build.

### Examples of Dual Consumer/B2B Models in Giving

**Bright Funds** is the clearest example: it built a consumer giving platform first, then added a workplace giving module. The consumer product gave it credibility with employees; the B2B product gave it revenue. It now positions as "the next generation platform for individual and workplace giving." This is the exact model GoodLocal could pursue — except Bright Funds targets national companies and GoodLocal's differentiation is the *local* angle.

**Millie** started as workplace-giving-first (B2B), but the consumer element (employees getting a personal giving experience) is part of the product design. The employer pays; the employee gets an app.

**Benevity** started as a CSR enterprise platform and built a consumer-facing nonprofit portal (causes.benevity.org) as an acquisition channel for the enterprise side. The consumer portal is a means to enterprise revenue, not a standalone business.

The pattern across all three: consumer and B2B are most compatible when the consumer product *is* the employee-facing interface for the B2B product. That is exactly GoodLocal's structure.

### Minimum Viable B2B Feature Set (Pilot with 3–5 Denver Companies)

To run a meaningful pilot, GoodLocal needs:

1. **Employer dashboard** — Company admin logs in, sets per-employee annual matching cap (e.g., $100/employee), sees matching budget consumed vs. available
2. **Employee invitation flow** — Admin sends invite link; employees join with work email or personal email
3. **Curated local nonprofit roster** — 15–25 Denver nonprofits pre-vetted; employer can optionally add or remove specific orgs
4. **Matching logic** — Employee donates via GoodLocal; system records it; employer is notified to approve match (manual approval for pilot is fine) or automatic up to cap
5. **Impact summary** — Monthly or quarterly summary showing: total matched, number of employees who participated, which nonprofits received funds
6. **Every.org integration** — Donations flow via existing Every.org widget; matching is tracked separately in GoodLocal's DB

**What is NOT required for the pilot:**
- Payroll integration
- Custom branding/white label
- Advanced reporting API
- Volunteer tracking
- Grant management
- Multi-language support

The pilot can be operated with significant manual process behind the scenes. The employer dashboard can be a simple admin view; match approvals can be email-based. The point is to validate willingness to pay and employee engagement, not to build a scalable enterprise platform.

**Estimated build scope:** 4–8 weeks of focused development for a functioning pilot, using GoodLocal's existing Every.org integration, authentication, and charity database as foundations.

### Phased Path: Consumer-Only to B2B-Enabled

**Phase 0 — Now (Consumer Only)**
GoodLocal is live. Consumers can discover Denver nonprofits, donate via Every.org, track giving history. No B2B capability.

**Phase 1 — B2B Research and Validation (1–2 months)**
Identify 5–10 Denver companies in the Pledge 1% / B Corp network. Have founder-to-founder conversations. Validate: would they pay $300–$500/month? What's the minimum they need from the product? What's their biggest fear (admin burden, employee adoption, budget uncertainty)?

**Phase 2 — Manual B2B Pilot (2–3 months)**
Sign 3–5 pilot companies at reduced/free rate. Build the minimum employer dashboard. Operate matching verification manually. Collect data: employee activation rate, donation amounts, employer NPS, renewal intent.

**Phase 3 — Paid B2B Tier Launch (month 6+)**
With proof of concept from pilot, launch paid tiers. Formalize pricing. Build self-serve onboarding. Begin warm outreach to Pledge 1% Colorado, B Local Colorado, and Built In Colorado companies.

**Phase 4 — Channel Partnerships (month 9–12)**
Approach PEOs, Denver Chamber, HR consultants. Explore a revenue-share or referral arrangement. At 20–30 customers, GoodLocal has the traction to attract channel partners.

**Phase 5 — Consumer-B2B Flywheel**
B2B employees who engage with GoodLocal for their employer benefit become personal users. Consumer retention and B2B renewal reinforce each other. This is the long-term moat: GoodLocal is where Denver gives, whether you found it through your employer or on your own.

## Key Takeaways and Recommendations

1. **The B2B opportunity is real and underserved.** Only 28% of SMBs offer matching today vs. 65% of large companies. The enterprise tools are inaccessible; the lightweight alternatives have no local-first angle. GoodLocal's combination of local curation + simple pricing + existing donation infrastructure is a genuine gap.

2. **Groundswell ($4,500/year) is the most direct comparable for pricing.** GoodLocal should price below it (e.g., $299/month Starter) while offering the local-curation differentiation Groundswell lacks.

3. **Denver has a uniquely warm first-customer pool.** Pledge 1% Colorado (250 companies) and B Local Colorado (100+ B Corps) are pre-qualified buyers who have publicly opted into "giving as corporate identity." Start there. Don't cold-pitch chamber of commerce members; sell to the people who already believe.

4. **The buyer is one person, not a committee.** Under 100 employees, this is a CEO or Head of People decision. Under $10,000/year, it doesn't need legal or procurement. The sales cycle should be 2–4 weeks with a 30-day trial.

5. **Build the pilot before building the product.** Run 3–5 companies manually. Learn what they actually use. The employer dashboard, impact summary, and match approval flow should be informed by real usage before investing in a polished build.

6. **The consumer and B2B products are synergistic, not competing.** GoodLocal's consumer app is the employee-facing interface for the B2B product. Every B2B customer potentially creates consumer users. The key risk is trying to build both to production quality simultaneously — sequence it (consumer first to establish credibility, then B2B pilot, then B2B product).

7. **Avoid the % of donations model.** It conflicts with GoodLocal's trust positioning ("we don't touch the money") and will raise red flags with both employers and nonprofit partners. Charge for the platform, not for the philanthropy.
