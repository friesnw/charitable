# GoodLocal — Target Audience

## Primary Persona: The Denver Professional Who Wants to Give Better

### Who They Are

A Millennial or Gen Z professional living in Denver. They work in tech, software, design, product, finance, or a tech-adjacent field (consulting, healthcare tech, startup ops). They earn well above Denver's median income, often in the $90K–$300K range per household, but they don't think of themselves as wealthy. They own their financial decisions deliberately.

They are not new to Denver. They chose Denver — for the lifestyle, the outdoors, the community, the startup culture. They care about the city in a way that a transplant who just landed for a job might not. Denver feels like home, and they have opinions about it.

---

### Demographics

- **Age:** 25–40
- **Location:** Denver proper — Capitol Hill, RiNo, LoDo, Five Points, Highland, Cheesman Park
- **Occupation:** Software engineer, product manager, designer, data analyst, startup founder, consultant, healthcare tech, fintech, creative agency
- **Income:** $90K–$250K individual; dual-income households often $150K–$450K
- **Education:** College-educated; often graduate degree or self-taught technical background
- **Housing:** Renting or recently purchased a first home — financially active, not casual

---

### Psychographics

**Values-driven, but skeptical of how to act on it**
They care about inequality, housing, food access, kids in their city. But they've learned to be skeptical — of campaigns, of viral fundraising moments, of organizations that feel opaque or political. They want to give in a way that feels honest and aligned with what they actually believe, not what they were pressured into or a charitable fundraiser that doesn't feel genuinely focused on the charity and impact itself.

**Financially conscious — even with money**
They have disposable income, but they don't throw it around. They track spending. They have a Roth IRA and probably an ETF portfolio. They think about their financial decisions with intention. They could donate $100 without blinking, but they want to feel like that $100 actually matters before they hand it over.

**Professionally anchored, personally disconnected**
Many of them spend 8+ hours a day at a screen, in a job that feels abstract. They contribute to products or companies that are far removed from their local community. That creates a quiet dissonance — they're doing fine personally, but they're not sure they're doing anything that matters close to home. GoodLocal is the relief valve for that feeling.

**Experience-seekers with a neighborhood mindset**
They go to local breweries, farmers markets, independent coffee shops. They care about which businesses they support locally. That same sensibility extends — at least in theory — to charitable giving. The idea of supporting an organization a few miles away, where they can see the impact, resonates more than writing a check to a national brand.

**Civic but not political**
They vote. They stay informed. But they've grown tired of politics as the primary way to make change. They're looking for alternatives that feel personal and effective rather than partisan and performative. GoodLocal sits in that gap — apolitical, local, tangible.

---

### Their Relationship with Giving Today

Most of them give inconsistently. They might donate to one or two organizations — often because a friend asked them to, or because of a moment (a news story, a Colorado Gives Day prompt). Their giving is reactive, not habitual. They don't have a giving budget; they have giving guilt.

They've never felt fully confident in a donation:
- Is this organization legit?
- Is my $50 going to overhead or actually helping someone?
- Would I be better off volunteering time instead?
- How does this compare to just writing a check to a bigger, more famous charity?

Those questions don't get answered by most giving experiences. So they either donate impulsively (and forget about it) or hesitate long enough that the moment passes.

---

### Their Friction Points

| Friction                  | What It Feels Like                                                                             |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Trust**                 | "I've never heard of this org. How do I know it's real?"                                       |
| **Impact clarity**        | "Does my $50 actually do anything, or does it disappear into overhead?"                        |
| **Discovery**             | "I don't know which nonprofits are working on [issue] in Denver specifically."                 |
| **Affordability anxiety** | "I want to give, but I don't want to commit to something I can't sustain."                     |
| **Scattered history**     | "I donated to three places last year and I have no idea what I gave in total."                 |
| **National vs. local**    | "The Red Cross feels too big and generic. Is there something more local I can actually trust?" |

---

### What They Respond To

- **Honesty over hype.** They have a finely tuned bullshit detector. If it feels like marketing, it loses them. If it feels like a real person wrote it, they lean in.
- **Proximity and specificity.** "A Denver nonprofit serving families in Five Points" lands better than "a national organization helping communities." They want to picture where their money goes.
- **Calm, not urgent.** Urgency-based giving appeals feel manipulative to them. They respond better to information and evidence than pressure.
- **Financial framing.** Showing that $10/month = $120/year = concrete impact X resonates. Treat them like they understand math and want to.
- **Local peers and community signals.** They trust recommendations from people in their network more than national credibility signals. A friend's endorsement of a Denver nonprofit beats a charity rating from a national site.

---

### What They Explicitly Don't Want

- Guilt trips or emotional manipulation
- Leaderboards or public comparison of giving amounts
- Pressure to give more or faster than feels comfortable
- Vague, unmeasurable impact claims ("your donation changes lives")
- Being marketed to after donating
- A product that feels like a fundraising tool for charities rather than a tool for them

---

### How They Discover Things Like This

- **Peer recommendation** — A friend or coworker mentions it in conversation or shares it on Slack/Discord
- **Denver tech community** — Denver Tech Meetup, Denver Startup Week, coworking spaces like Commons on Champa
- **Local subreddits and communities** — r/Denver, Nextdoor, neighborhood Facebook groups
- **Google search** — "best nonprofits in Denver," "where to donate in Denver" when the intention is already there
- **Colorado Gives Day** — The one moment per year when Denver giving culture is activated at scale
- **Content** — A well-written "Denver Giving Guide" or nonprofit spotlight that shows up where they already are

---

### Their Relationship with Technology

They are comfortable with technology and expect it to work without explanation. They are suspicious of apps that feel "too app-y" — over-gamified, notification-heavy, or demanding. The apps they trust feel clean, intentional, and focused. They compare GoodLocal (in their mental model) to tools like Robinhood, Monarch Money, Notion — things that treat them as capable adults making real decisions.

Magic link login will not confuse them. A thoughtful map interface is an asset. Donation via Apple Pay is expected. Clutter, pop-ups, and dark patterns are dealbreakers.

---

### Why GoodLocal Is Right for Them (And Not Someone Else)

GoodLocal is not for the donor who gives to national causes because their employer matches it. It's not for the person writing annual large checks to a hospital's fundraising gala. It's not for the hyperactive social donor who turns giving into a public identity statement.

It's for the person who genuinely wants to do something good, close to home, in a way that makes sense financially and feels worth repeating. They want to feel like a thoughtful participant in their city's wellbeing — not a one-time transaction.

That person is a Denver tech professional under 40. And right now, they don't have a product built for them.



# Audience Top Neighborhoods

These are the Denver neighborhoods with the highest concentration of GoodLocal's target audience — young professionals aged 25–40 with above-average incomes. Ranked by relevance to the target persona.

---

## Tier 1 — Highest Concentration

### Highland (LoHi)
- **Population:** ~10,700
- **Median age:** 34–36
- **25–44 age group:** 54.8% of residents — the highest of any Denver neighborhood
- **Median household income:** $114,028 | Average: $156,230
- **93% of working residents** are in professional or administrative roles
- **Character:** Young families, couples, and solo professionals. Walkable, restaurant-dense, close to downtown without being downtown. Strong community identity. Increasingly upscale.
- **Relevance:** The single densest concentration of our target user. High income, high professional density, strong neighborhood identity — ideal for word-of-mouth and hyperlocal marketing.

---

### LoDo (Lower Downtown)
- **Population:** ~18,200
- **Median age:** 34
- **25–44 age group:** 52.3% of residents
- **Median housing cost:** $2,231/month — signals high income
- **Character:** Urban core. Condo dwellers, transplants who chose Denver's downtown lifestyle. Dense, walkable, nightlife-forward but increasingly professional.
- **Relevance:** High density, young, financially capable. The tech and finance professional who chose downtown living. Strong for digital channels and in-person events.

---

### RiNo (River North Art District)
- **Population:** ~5,350
- **Median age:** 32 — the youngest of any major Denver neighborhood
- **25–44 age group:** 48.3% of residents
- **Median housing cost:** $1,925/month
- **Character:** Creative, tech-forward, startup culture. Art studios, breweries, coffee shops. The neighborhood most likely to contain someone who'd describe themselves as "working in tech or something adjacent."
- **Relevance:** Culturally aligned with the brand. Home to the creative-class professional who is values-driven but not necessarily traditional. Strong for grassroots and physical marketing (coasters, stickers, events).

---

## Tier 2 — Strong Secondary

### Capitol Hill
- **Population:** ~12,200
- **Median age:** 34
- **Character:** Dense, renter-heavy, politically engaged. Mix of young professionals, artists, and longtime residents. The most diverse of the target neighborhoods by lifestyle and income.
- **Relevance:** High volume of target-adjacent users. More likely to be earlier in their career or income trajectory, but values-aligned. Strong grassroots activation potential via Nextdoor and community events.

---

### Sloan's Lake
- **Population:** ~8,500
- **Median age:** 35
- **Median household income:** $118,188 | Average: $151,832
- **25–44 residents earn:** $148,074 median
- **Character:** Up-and-coming. Walkable around the lake, mix of longtime residents and newer arrivals. Increasingly popular with professionals priced out of Highland. Quieter lifestyle with strong outdoor identity.
- **Relevance:** Growing rapidly in target demographic. Income profile matches well. Strong for word-of-mouth and neighborhood-level social channels.

---

### Cherry Creek
- **Population:** Mixed residential/commercial
- **Median age:** 37
- **25–44 age group:** 46.2% of residents
- **Median household income:** $110,504 | Average: $159,774
- **25–44 median income:** $115,048
- **Character:** Upscale, polished, shopping district with growing residential density. Slightly older skew than the core target, but high-income and philanthropically active.
- **Relevance:** High income, slightly older but still in range. More likely to already be giving somewhere — opportunity to win them over from national/large charity habits.

---

### Washington Park
- **Population:** Established residential
- **Median age:** 36
- **25–44 age group:** 41.3% of residents
- **Median household income:** $119,458 | Average: $183,856
- **25–44 median income:** $139,570 — highest of any neighborhood listed
- **Character:** Family-oriented, settled professionals. Parks, outdoor lifestyle, strong community feel. Homeowners more than renters. Slightly older skew but the highest income profile.
- **Relevance:** High income and strong community values make this a natural fit, though slightly less tech-saturated. Reaches the persona who has recently settled down and is thinking about giving as part of a stable adult life.

---

### Five Points
- **Population:** ~8,350
- **Median age:** 34
- **Character:** Historic neighborhood adjacent to RiNo, rapidly gentrifying. Mix of longtime community members and newer creative/professional arrivals. Vibrant arts and music scene.
- **Relevance:** Close cultural overlap with RiNo. Relevant for both target users and for the nonprofits likely to serve the community — creating a natural connection between GoodLocal's audience and the causes they'd most want to support nearby.
